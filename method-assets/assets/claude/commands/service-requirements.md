# /service-requirements

> The Requirements step of a service activity: capture what one component is obligated to do, who calls it, and what quality bars it must clear — before any contract is drafted.

**Arguments** — `$ARGUMENTS` is `<component_id> <activity_id>` (two space-separated tokens; `component_id` may be empty for non-component activities). Parse once; do not swap them. Work lands on the shared activity branch `activity/<activity_id>` and its single PR — you are contributing commits to a PR that already exists (or open it if this is the first phase). Do NOT open a second PR.

**Agent + skills.** Work to the standard of the **`senior-developer`** agent (`.claude/agents/senior-developer.md`). Follow **[[the-method-service-contract]]** and **[[the-method-project-state]]** for all reading/updating of `.aiarch/state/project.json`.

**Goal / intention.** Per Löwy's per-service life cycle (App A Table A-1), Requirements is the first internal phase of a service activity, distinct from and lighter-weight than Detailed Design — it is not contract design. Ch. 9 explains what a senior developer's time is actually for: senior developers deliver services faster than juniors not because they type faster, but because they spend the bulk of their time designing the module, working out its interactions, and planning how they will test it — contemplating the implications of each decision and weighing maintainability, extensibility, and security before committing to an approach. Ch. 7 warns against scoping a service by its coding effort alone: examine the full scope of the work ahead — the learning curve, integration points, test clients, documentation — because misjudging scope is what produces the two classic failure modes, an overestimated activity that invites gold-plating, or an underestimated one that invites cut corners. The point of this step is to pin down precisely what this one component is obligated to do, who calls it and why, and what quality bars it must clear, so that Detailed Design can move fast and correctly instead of guessing. "Done" means the component's responsibilities, callers, and constraints are captured — not that any interface has been drafted. This step must NOT propose operations or a contract shape (that is Detailed Design's job) and must NOT write code.

## Steps

1. **Read what you need** from `.aiarch/state/project.json` per [[the-method-project-state]]: the activity, its component's entry in the committed system design (layer, relationships in/out), the volatility it encapsulates, and its sync/queued + pub/sub treatment in the committed operational concepts.
2. **Produce** the phase artifact: a scope note for the component — its responsibilities, its callers/consumers, its non-functional constraints (maintainability, extensibility, security), and open questions for the next phase — recorded into the phase-artifacts store via `recordPhaseArtifact` per [[the-method-project-state]] and committed onto branch `activity/<activity_id>`.
3. **Verify** (only your own output; fast checks): every responsibility you captured traces to an actual relationship or dynamic-view edge in the committed system design — no invented scope; no contract operations or interface shapes are proposed; the note reads as scope capture, not as a design document.
4. **Stop.** Do not mark phase status (the Manager owns that) and do not merge. Leave the PR open for the gate.
